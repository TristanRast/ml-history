# The First AI Winter (1969-1980)
Before the success of Rosenblatt's Perceptron, a conference was held in the summer of 1956 at Dartmouth College, New Hampshire. This conference would later be called the "Constitutional Convention of AI" for its foundational discussions. 
From this workshop emerged two camps:  
**Marvin Minsky's Symbolic AI** that required encoding of human knowledge and skills with a rules-based system and  
**Neural Network Connectionists** that believed you should build systems that allowed machines to learn knowledge and skills from data using the human brain as a model.  

Minsky, the "godfather of AI," devised a kill shot for the rival camp. In 1969, 9 years after the unveiling of Rosenblatt's Perceptron, he and Seymour Papert published *Perceptrons*, which proved mathematically that single-layer perceptrons **cannot** learn XOR. XOR is a simple function requiring nonlinear separation unlike the linear decision boundaries that the Connectionists had been building.  
The claim hit hard: DARPA, the primary funding source for AI research at the time, began cutting funding from neural net research. Academic journals stopped accepting neural net papers, and Frank Rosenblatt died in a boating accident just two years later, leaving the Connectionists without their most popular pioneer.

The **First AI Winter** (1974-1980) devastated the field. DARPA withdrew support for general AI. The irony was that Minsky's proof applied only to *single-layer* perceptrons. Summer was coming for neural nets but they needed one major breakthrough. They needed to learn how to train with multiple layers.
